# Agent Probe / Case 026 / Ideas Change Cards — Style-Family Spec

## Product role decision
**Case 026 should become an argument-through-cards surface: a thought-leadership or advocacy page where a series of concise idea cards carries the product’s thesis rather than a long essay or dashboard.**

This is a card-based proposition system.

---

## Why this mode fits the source
- The title suggests **ideas** and **cards**, indicating modular claims rather than one monolithic narrative.
- “Ideas change” implies a product that persuades through **a sequence of conceptual units**.
- Card structures are excellent for turning abstract positions into **digestible, ordered statements**.
- The likely composition values repetition with variation: each card has a role in a broader argument.
- This mode is stronger as **structured persuasion** than as utility software.

---

## Style-family thesis
This family works for:
- advocacy explainers
- strategic principle pages
- manifesto products
- educational campaigns
- editorial concept decks
- research insight summaries

It is good at:
- chunking arguments
- sequential persuasion
- flexible scanning
- combining concept and proof

It is bad for:
- real-time operations
- dense data reporting
- large archives
- pure poster singularity

---

## Non-negotiable visual locks
1. **Repeated cards must structure the argument.** Each card should express one idea, not many.
2. **The cards must feel related but not identical in purpose.** Use type, order, or scale to distinguish thesis, evidence, example, and action.
3. **Copy should stay concise and idea-led.** Cards are for claims, prompts, or principles — not long paragraphs.
4. **The layout must preserve momentum across the set.** Users should feel a sequence rather than isolated modules.
5. **The visual tone should support conviction.** The design must feel purposeful enough to carry a position.

---

## Concept probe suggestion
**Why Public Tools Matter** — an advocacy page for civic software funding built from a series of concise cards covering access, trust, infrastructure, and long-term value.

---

## Acceptance checklist
- The page reads as a **sequence of persuasive idea cards**.
- Each card holds one focused conceptual unit.
- The set creates a clear argumentative arc.
- The design makes scanning easy without flattening importance.
- Copy remains compact and memorable.
- The concept plausibly supports real advocacy or thought leadership.

---

## Failure tests
- **Fail if** the cards are interchangeable and lack distinct roles.
- **Fail if** the page turns into a generic blog summary.
- **Fail if** the argument is so diffuse that no thesis emerges.

- **Pass if** the cards collectively make a clear case.
- **Pass if** a reviewer can recall several distinct ideas after scanning.
- **Pass if** the interface feels like a designed position, not a content dump.
